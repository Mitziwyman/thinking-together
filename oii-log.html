What the OII is

The Organisational Intelligence Index makes visible what is often less visible: how value shifts across teams, across the system, and over time as a result of decisions that are made. It focuses not on intent, but on what actually shapes decisions under operational pressure.

Most organisations measure what is easy to count — financial performance, activity, compliance. The OII starts from a different question:

What was actually visible when the decisions affecting that value were being made?

The focus is less on flows of capital and organisational outputs, and more on attention, judgement, trade-offs, and what was present — or absent — in the room when decisions were taken.

The intellectual centre

The sentence that holds the idea:

The OII is not primarily about value creation. It is about the visibility of value at the point decisions are made.

This is what differentiates it from both traditional governance diagnostics and Integrated Reporting.

Relationship to Integrated Reporting

Integrated Reporting asks what forms of value are being created, preserved or eroded. The OII asks what was visible when the decisions affecting that value were being made.

Where Integrated Reporting focuses on flows of capital and value creation, the OII focuses on attention, visibility, judgement, and the conditions under which decisions are made. It shifts the enquiry from outputs to the room where decisions happened.

What it asks

  • What was being attended to?
  • What was difficult to see?
  • What was difficult to raise?
  • Which forms of value were present in the room?
  • Which forms of value were absent?

Where it sits

The OII sits at the intersection of governance, board effectiveness, leadership, organisational psychology, decision-making, integrated reporting, and systems thinking. It is not sector-specific — it applies wherever senior people make consequential decisions under pressure.

The six forms of value

  • Financial resources
  • People and capability
  • Relationships and trust
  • Knowledge and expertise
  • Community context
  • Natural environment

The five patterns of decision-making

  • Compressed Horizon — short-term focus; longer-term consequences largely unseen
  • Well-Managed Silo — strong within boundaries; value shifting between teams unnoticed
  • Articulate Avoider — values clearly expressed, but not consistently lived in decisions
  • Emerging Integrator — broader awareness growing, but not yet consistent
  • The Long View — operational and long-term considerations held together with clarity

The patterns are mirrors, not labels. They describe something that is already happening — not a category, not a judgement. They are starting points for a different kind of conversation.

Working tensions

These are the live tensions in the work — not problems to solve, but things to hold. Updated in a working session when something genuinely shifts.

Depth vs. reach

The OII's value lies in facilitated conversation — the report opens the door, but the real insight comes from what happens in the room. Scaling the tool without losing that quality is an unresolved tension.

NHS roots vs. wider application

The OII was developed in and for the NHS and public sector. The intellectual framework is not sector-specific, but the language, examples, and relationships are. Moving into governance and commercial contexts requires care about what travels and what doesn't.

Tool vs. framework

The OII could be positioned as a diagnostic tool (a thing you do) or as a conceptual framework (a way of thinking about organisational decision-making). The two are related but pull in different directions commercially and intellectually.

IP and openness

The ideas in the OII are shareable and generative. But sharing them freely, especially with well-connected people who operate at scale, carries risk. The right level of protection — licensing, partnerships, professional relationships — is not yet settled.

MII and OII — the relationship

The MII and OII are complementary but distinct. The MII focuses on what happens in meetings; the OII focuses on what shapes decisions across the organisation. The temptation to conflate them, or to position the OII as simply a bigger MII, would understate what the OII is.

Visibility and readiness

Taking the OII out more widely before it is fully formed carries risk. But waiting until it feels finished may mean missing strategic conversations at exactly the moment they are available. Paul Druckman's engagement in June 2026 surfaced this tension directly.
