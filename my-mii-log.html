What the MII is

Most organisations know their meetings could be better. What is less often examined is what that actually costs — in the quality of decisions made, in organisational energy, and in what meetings reveal about the culture of the place itself.

Under pressure, meetings default to information rather than inquiry. Thinking becomes compressed. Challenge becomes quieter. Over time, people learn what can be said — and what is better left unsaid. Decisions are made and actions followed through, but the question remains:

To what extent are those decisions drawing on the full intelligence available in the room?

The Meeting Intelligence Index makes that visible — quickly, precisely, and without a lot of overhead.

What it does

A structured diagnostic (~10 minutes) completed individually. Each person receives an individual report — not a generic summary, but a considered reading of what their particular combination of responses suggests about their meeting culture. Used with a team, the range between individuals is often the most revealing number.

The four dimensions

DimensionWhat it measures
Preparation & StructureWhether the infrastructure exists and creates conditions for genuine thinking — not just functional threshold
Participation & ListeningWhether all voices are fully in the room; whether contributions carry equal weight; whether the meeting operates with its full intelligence
Quality of ThinkingWhether discussions generate new thinking or stay close to the familiar; whether conditions exist for deeper inquiry
Decisions & Follow-ThroughWhether decisions get made and are held; whether execution is in place

Key insight

A high Decisions & Follow-Through score alongside low Participation and Thinking scores is a specific, important pattern: things are moving, but the conditions that would make that movement wiser are not yet in place. Strong execution applied to decisions made without full thinking is efficient — but carries a cost that builds over time.

Facilitator principle

The data makes questions visible; it cannot answer them. The real value is in facilitated conversation — space to explore what sustains the patterns and what it would take to shift them.

Working tensions

These are the live tensions in the work — not problems to solve, but things to hold. Updated in a working session when something genuinely shifts.

Individual insight vs. team conversation

The individual report is valuable. But the real moment is when a team looks at the range between them — whose experience has been shaping meeting design, and who has quietly learned not to expect more. Getting to that conversation requires trust and skilled facilitation. The tool opens the door; it cannot guarantee what happens next.

Healthy teams and the unremarkable report

In well-functioning teams, MII reports are less dramatic. This is not a limitation — it is accurate. But it creates a challenge when introducing the tool to high-performing leaders: the report may not surface the tension needed to justify the conversation. Paul Druckman's June 2026 trial illustrated this directly.

Tool vs. conversation starter

The MII could be positioned as a standalone diagnostic (a thing you do once) or as the opening move in an ongoing conversation about meeting culture. The two are not incompatible, but they require different framing and different follow-through. Most of the commercial value lives in the second version.

IP and commercial scale

The MII is already live and in use. The question is not whether it works, but how far it travels. Board Intelligence and similar governance consultancies could take it to scale — but that introduces questions about licensing, branding, and what Mitzi's ongoing role would be. Paul Druckman's instinct to flag IP was well-founded.

MII and OII — keeping them distinct

The MII focuses on what happens in meetings. The OII focuses on what shapes decisions across the organisation. They are complementary and sequential — better meetings lead to better organisational decisions — but they are not the same thing. Marketing them together risks blurring both.

NHS roots vs. governance market

The MII was built in and for the NHS and public sector. The dynamics it surfaces — compressed thinking, unspoken assumptions, unequal voices — are recognisable anywhere, but the language and examples are grounded in public sector experience. Entering the board governance and commercial sector requires care about translation.
